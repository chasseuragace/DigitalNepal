## The hard truth about the infrastructure gap

**On the hardware side**, Nepal's single most dangerous constraint is internet architecture, not power. The power problem is real but solvable in phases. The internet problem is structural: when roughly 95% of your international traffic transits through one neighboring country, you don't have a tech economy — you have a dependency. An IXP (Internet Exchange Point) in Kathmandu, like the ones that transformed Nairobi and Lagos into African tech hubs, would let local traffic stay local and reduce latency dramatically. This costs roughly $3–8 million to build and probably 2 years of political negotiation to route around BSNL's de facto monopoly on transit peering.

The data centre gap is also serious but not the first problem. Nepal has no Tier-3 certified facility. The closest thing is NTC's datacenter in Bhotahiti, which doesn't meet international uptime SLAs. Any serious cloud or fintech company evaluating Nepal needs at least a Tier-3 facility in Kathmandu and ideally a geographically separate DR site — Pokhara or Birgunj. A single anchor Tier-3 DC of 5–10 MW capacity costs roughly NPR 4–8 billion. That's not an impossible number if it's structured as a PPP with a serious private operator.

**On the software side**, the absolute foundation — before any portal, any one-stop service, any cooperative digitization — is a working national identity API. The NID card rollout is roughly 60% complete but it's a card, not a system. Converting that into an API that developers can use to verify citizens is a 12–18 month software project. India did the equivalent with Aadhaar in 18 months and it unlocked an entire fintech ecosystem (PhonePe, Paytm, CRED all run on Aadhaar-based KYC). Without this, every digitization effort rebuilds identity verification from scratch — which is exactly what's happening now with each ministry running its own login system.

The NRB fintech sandbox is the other quick win. It requires zero hardware and minimal software — it's a regulatory framework that says "here are 10 companies that can experiment with FX-linked digital payments for 18 months under waived rules." Pakistan did this and had Stripe operational within 14 months. The political resistance is the only real obstacle; the technical work is straightforward.

The digital land registry is the longest and hardest but ultimately the most consequential for the RSP's agricultural and cooperative reform agenda. You cannot consolidate agricultural land (the manifesto's `ल्यान्ड कन्सोलिडेसन` promise) without knowing who owns what. You cannot use land as collateral for cooperative loans without verified digital title. Ethiopia built a district-level land registry with SIDA funding for about $30 million covering 20 million parcels. Nepal has roughly 5 million parcels — it's a 3–5 year project if started today, not a 1-year one.

The honest framing: **priorities 1 and 2 above can be completed inside 18 months for under NPR 500 million combined.** Priority 3 is 2 years and NPR 1–2 billion. Priority 4 is 5 years and NPR 3–6 billion. None of these require the hydropower megaprojects or the data centre dreams. They're the base layer that makes every other promise in the manifesto actually possible to deliver.
